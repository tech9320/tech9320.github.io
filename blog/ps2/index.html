Sony: Trailblazing Ad Campaigns

Before the rise of the digital age and the widespread influence of the internet on pop culture, printed media and advertisements held significant sway in promoting ideas and brands. Aimed at comic book fans, magazine readers, and customers of second-hand bookshops, printed ads remained effective. In 1996, PlayStation capitalized on this by distributing a flyer at the Glastonbury Festival to showcase its groundbreaking console, the PlayStation 1. Alongside the image of the PlayStation was the slogan “More Powerful than God,” sparking controversy and hinting at the provocative advertising strategies PlayStation would continue to use in the years to follow.

In addition to pioneering advancements in interactive 3D technology, PlayStation played a role in the creation of games targeted at a more mature audience. Titles such as Max Payne, Grand Theft Auto III, and Metal Gear, among others, embraced design influences reminiscent of late-nineties club aesthetics, corporate grunge, and GEN X Soft-Club designs. These aesthetics significantly influenced PS2 print advertisements, evoking the cinematic atmospheres of films like Trainspotting, The Matrix, and Requiem for a Dream. For example, one PS2 ad featured a rain-soaked car, reminiscent of Neo’s encounter with Trinity’s vehicle in The Matrix (1999). The tagline “Different Place, Different Rules” frequently appeared on PS2 promotional and printed materials during the late 1990s and early 2000s.

The approach aimed to contrast everyday situations with surreal elements, drawing from punk and industrial styles and dystopian settings popularized by underground club movements and films like Fight Club (1999) and Blade (1998). Through these visuals, Sony’s PlayStation sought to attract an older audience, signaling that gaming was not just for a particular age group but could be enjoyed by everyone, including those with unconventional or eccentric tastes.

Sony injected an element of danger, darkness, and eccentricity into what was once seen as a mundane activity: playing video games. During the late 1990s and early 2000s, as corporate culture surged due to the widespread adoption of computers and the internet, concerns about stagnant careers, loss of personal drive, and navigating office politics became widespread. PlayStation’s advertising campaign tapped into this cultural atmosphere, reigniting a sense of excitement among workers and gamers alike. The influence of PlayStation’s pioneering promotion can still be sensed in the modern gaming aesthetic, serving as a reminder of its rebellious approach before conforming to subtlety and corporate norms…
